Abstract

Multi-camera capture — motion-capture stages, light-stage domes, phone arrays — depends on every camera's shutter firing at the same instant, yet recent large datasets report synchronized capture without ever measuring the residual error. We present a small, self-built LED timecode panel that makes that error directly observable: a flat panel of LEDs displays a fast-advancing, Gray-coded timecode that every camera decodes per frame, so the difference between what two cameras read at the same instant is precisely their synchronization error. The rig is neat, affordable, and customizable, and currently resolves inter-camera offset to sub-millisecond precision (0.5 ms). Beyond measuring today's rigs, it can serve as a reference signal for developing event-based synchronization methods.

1  Introduction

Multi-camera rigs only work if every camera's shutter fires at the same instant. Yet recent large capture datasets report synchronized capture without ever measuring the error: HumanOLAT [1] (40 cameras) and MVHumanNet [2] (48 cameras) both claim synchronization but publish no number for it. Without a measurement a sync-quality claim is unverifiable — so a trustworthy evaluation tool is the prerequisite for any work that hopes to improve synchronization.

We build such a tool. A row of LEDs shows a fast-advancing timecode — a number that ticks up thousands of times a second; every camera pointed at the panel reads that number off each frame it captures, and comparing what two cameras read at the same moment reveals how far apart their shutters actually fired. Our contributions are: (i) a low-cost, self-built LED timecode panel that measures inter-camera synchronization directly from the captured video; (ii) a latched, Gray-coded design that stays robust when a frame's exposure straddles a transition; and (iii) a working demonstration resolving inter-camera offset to 0.5 ms on a two-camera rig.

2  Related Work

The closest analogue is libsoftwaresync [3], a 10×10 LED panel that encodes time by position on the grid and reaches ~200 µs. Other LED-based rigs (e.g. Twist-n-Sync [4]) and camera-side post-processing (subframe alignment [5]) target a different setting or act at the wrong layer — aligning already-recorded video rather than the capture itself. None is a drop-in evaluation tool for a small, reconfigurable multi-camera rig, which is the gap this panel fills.

3  System Design

3.1  A minimal test: blink one LED

The smallest possible test: the microcontroller (a Raspberry Pi Pico) drives one LED through one resistor to ground (Figure 1). If it blinks, the code, the board, and the wiring are all sound — a foundation to build on.

3.2  Driving seven LEDs with a shift register

To drive several LEDs from just a few pins, the Pico feeds a shift-register chip, which turns three control wires into eight independent on/off outputs. Seven of those outputs each light an LED through a 240 Ω resistor, and the seven on/off states together form one number: the timecode. That number is shown in Gray code — a counting scheme in which only one LED changes from one step to the next — so a frame that happens to catch a change still reads a clean value (off by at most one), never a scrambled one. Figure 2 gives the schematic and Figure 3 the breadboard build.

4  Evaluation

4.1  What the two cameras filmed

Before the analysis, the raw input. These are the last 100 frames of a two-camera clip, with each camera's frame paired to the other camera's nearest frame in time and played side by side, so every pair shows the same moment seen twice. The timecode steps every 0.5 ms here (the panel's fastest setting), and both cameras film with a matching 0.5 ms exposure — short enough to freeze each fast-changing pattern into a clean, readable frame. The lit pattern tracks together across both views; the small residual difference is what the decode measures (Figure 4).

4.2  Inter-camera offset across the clip

With two cameras pointed at the running panel, every frame each one captures carries a readable timecode, so the gap between what the two cameras read at the same instant is how far apart their shutters actually fired: their synchronization error. We measure it not once but at six points spread across a full 60 s clip — five frames around each 10 s mark. The offset is the same everywhere, about +2 ms: a stable, repeatable error, not drift (Figure 5).

5  Conclusion and Future Work

We have built and validated a compact LED timecode panel that makes inter-camera synchronization error directly measurable, demonstrating 0.5 ms resolution on a two-camera rig. The immediate next step is scaling the evaluation to the full 11-camera Pixel 7 / Argus rig, targeting millisecond resolution at that scale. Because the panel emits a fast, decodable timing signal, it also makes a natural reference for developing and benchmarking event-based synchronization methods.

Appendix A  Glossary

Every technical term and diagram label above, in plain language.

TermMeaning
MicrocontrollerA tiny computer on a single chip. Here it’s a Raspberry Pi Pico, which runs the code that drives the LEDs.
Raspberry Pi PicoThe microcontroller board used — the “controller” in the diagrams.
BreadboardA reusable board for wiring circuits by pushing parts into rows of holes — no soldering.
LEDLight-emitting diode — the small lights that show the code.
Resistor (240 Ω)Limits the current through an LED so it lights safely; Ω (ohm) is the unit of resistance.
Shift register (74HC595)A chip that turns a few control wires into many on/off outputs — here, three wires from the Pico into eight outputs. The “driver chip.”
TimecodeA number that counts up at a steady, known rate. Read it off a camera frame and you know when that frame was captured.
Gray codeA way of counting in which only one bit (one LED) changes between consecutive values — so a frame catching a mid-change still reads a clean value, off by at most one.
Synchronization errorHow far apart two cameras’ shutters actually fired for “the same” frame — what the panel measures.
GP15, GP17–GP19Numbered general-purpose pins on the Pico. GP15 drives the single blink LED; GP17–GP19 carry the three control signals to the shift register.
SER, SRCLK, RCLKThe shift register’s three control inputs — SER: data in; SRCLK: shift clock (loads each bit); RCLK: latch clock (shows the whole byte on the outputs at once).
QA, QB–QHThe shift register’s eight outputs. QB–QH each light one LED (seven in all); QA is unused.
OE, MROutput Enable and Master Reset — tied to the levels that keep the outputs on and stop the chip from resetting.
VCC, GND, 3V3Power and ground: VCC = the chip’s supply; GND = 0 V (the common return); 3V3 = the Pico’s 3.3 V output, which powers the chip.
